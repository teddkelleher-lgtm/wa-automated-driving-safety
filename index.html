Washington Road Safety Counterfactual

How many Washington lives could wider automated driving save?

Washington is not a place where automated driving is blocked outright. The harder question is what continued slow adoption costs. This site models how many traffic deaths and serious injuries could be avoided if a larger share of vehicle miles in Washington were driven by systems that perform closer to current Waymo safety benchmarks than to human drivers.

    Methodology

    How the calculation works

    avoidable deaths = county deaths × automated share × 0.82
    • County deaths come from 2024 NHTSA FARS, grouped by Washington county using the FATALS field in accident.csv.
    • Statewide serious injuries come from the Washington Traffic Safety Commission, which reported 3,290 serious injuries in 2024.
    • The safety-effect parameter is fixed at 82%, matching Waymo’s current public claim for fewer injury-causing crashes versus the human benchmark. The same page reports 92% fewer serious injury or worse crashes, so this site uses the lower figure as the baseline.
    • The model assumes automated miles are spread across the state in proportion to today’s harm and that benefits scale linearly with market share. Reality will be messier, but the arithmetic is transparent and easy to audit.

    Policy Posture

    What this argues for in Washington

    Washington already allows automated-vehicle testing and operation through a self-certification regime. So the point here is not to claim that the state has a DC-style blockade. The point is that every year of slower deployment still carries a measurable human cost.

    A pro-safety posture would keep unnecessary barriers low, preserve room for commercial scaling, and consider neutral incentives that speed up adoption of demonstrably safer automated fleets. That does not require taking away the option for people to drive themselves. It means letting safer driving technology earn share faster where it proves safer.
